# How Nitor works

Habits are boring. Keeping them shouldn't be. Here's how Nitor's forgiving streaks, flexible history, and daily quotes actually work.

## Forgiving streaks

Life happens, and one missed day shouldn't erase weeks of effort. Nitor gives you two ways to stay forgiving with yourself.

**Grace days** are a small, per-week allowance you set yourself, habit by habit. If you already know Sundays are a wash, or you need a couple of flexible days built in, grace days cover them automatically — no questions asked.

**Streak-freezes** are different: you earn them. For every 7 scheduled days you complete, you earn one freeze, and you can bank up to 2 at a time. Think of them as a reward for consistency that's there when you need it most.

Freezes never spend themselves without asking. If you miss a single day and it's about to break a streak you've built, Nitor will check in and ask whether you'd like to use a freeze to protect it, or let the streak reset. You're always the one who decides. And if you don't have a freeze banked, Nitor won't nag you about it — it just moves on. One important note: a freeze can only rescue an isolated missed day, not two missed days in a row, so it stays a safety net rather than a way to coast.

## Fix any day

Open any habit and you'll see its story at a glance: a compact heatmap of your recent history, your current and longest streaks, your momentum, and how many freezes you currently have banked.

Forgot to check something off, or logged the wrong day by mistake? Head to the habit's History and you can correct any check-in from the last 7 days. Anything older than that is locked in, so your history stays honest while still giving you room to fix an honest mistake.

You can also edit a habit's details any time, and reorder your habits by dragging them into the order that makes sense to you — or with your keyboard, if you'd rather not drag.

## Quotes that stay fresh

Every day, Nitor shows you a quote to sit with. Each one is traced back to a real, verifiable source — no misattributed lines pulled from the internet, no guessing who actually said it.

The quotes rotate by date, so you won't see the same one again for a long while, and the collection keeps growing over time, so it keeps feeling new rather than repeating itself.
